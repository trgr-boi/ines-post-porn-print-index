This index functions as a navigational tool. It's a guide through a personal compilation of archival material related to the post-porn movement. It offers a starting point from which readers can explore, browse, and dive deeper into a variety of printed matter.

What began as a search for compelling archive material on sexuality and print culture quickly evolved into a personal obsession, and eventually became my master project for Graphic Design. This index does not live as an archive, but a curated entry point, an invitation to further research and dive deeper. I want to bring in a head start, already gathering the material for further investigation and projects. Its aim is to reintroduce the post-porn movement into a contemporary context, making space for it within current discourse.

At its core, this index stems from an urge to list, categorize, collect. It reflects an almost bureaucratic desire to order the world, but towards valuing overlooked, niche and forgotten material. Most of the content I found digging through the wonderful world of the internet, going through eBay, online stores, city archives, personal collections, other magazines, articles, etc. Eventually also my personal camera roll, friends who send me sources, and books and publications on underground print culture.

WHAT IS POST-PORN?

The post-porn movement is situated in a broader struggle against patriarchal systems. Since the 1970s, pornography has become a crucial space of analysis, critique and reappropriation for the micro-politics of gender, sex, race, and sexuality.[1] The post-pornographic movement looks for another way of knowing and producing pleasure through looking, but also a new definition of the public space.

Following what the pro-sex feminists stated, this movement argues that sexual imagery could be reclaimed and reimagined, rather than censored. Post-pornography is defined by three aspects: the denaturalizing of sex, the de-centering of the spectator and the recognition of media & technology as inseparable from sex.[2] Rather than centering commercial porn, post-porn uses sex and the body as vehicles for expression, critique, and liberation.

Even though it's a very influential movement, covering all sorts of thematics and appearing all over the world, it has been easily overlooked and forgotten.

WHAT THIS INDEX IS (AND WHAT NOT)

This index operates as a system of references, making it a tool for navigating information. The form of an index cannot be confused with this other one, the archive. They differ in both form and content. While the archive can be used as a way to store information, thus "saving it from oblivion", the index has a different purpose: to facilitate navigation through the existing data. While all archival structures process material, the index relies on the largest volume, dividing material into key concepts, creating an alphabetical order rather than chronological, and creating numerous links between separate elements through cross-referencing.[3] As Dennis Duncan said in 'Index, a history of the': "The alphabetic arrangement of the index enables one to locate what otherwise would most likely be buried in the chaotic excess of data. (...) The information is 'useless' if 'unarranged' but 'valuable', because searchable, when sorted alphabetically."[4]

According to Mindy Seu these two forms contrast in the way that "an archive points inwards, an index is essentially a finding aid, pointing outward. It brings together disparate objects in a porous container."[5] The index materializes a feminist approach to knowledge, creating a structure that is permeable and interconnected. An index can be seen as a collage, an assemblage of disconnected items all stitched together in this whole.

So, an index points, whereas an archive acts as a repository, a storage-room. The term "index" suggests a spatial relationship: pointing to something here will indicate something. In the semiotic sense, an index is a sign that points to something present but invisible. Read literally: the index as a finger, specifically the index finger, which points, indicates, interrupts. "There he is, seated on the left, his long index finger extended, the classic gesture of the storyteller."[6] In many patriarchal contexts, women are taught not to point, not to interrupt, not to claim. So, the "use of the index"—the finger, the pointing, the underlining—can be read symbolically as a reclaiming of authority and direction. A feminist gesture because you take up space, you point, and you command attention.

Indexing is nowadays variously understood as a gesture of organization, search, transformation, and reduction. Duncan proposes that "indexing" in the broadest sense, occurs whenever we organize the world around us so that we know where to find things. "At its most general, it is a system adopted as a timesaver, telling us where to look for things."[7]

This list is not complete. The structure is deliberately limited: entries are organized alphabetically, without alternative filtering systems. Because the information has already been filtered and curated—by me. Rather than striving for objective completeness, the index zooms in on a part of a canon: a partial, situated perspective on post-porn print culture. Making this a personal documentation of the history of the movement.

CENTRAL LENS AND CURATION

My focus for the material extends beyond gay and queer zines alone. The central lens is sex(uality) approached from a feminist perspective, foregrounding political voices and marginalized representations. Ranging from queer bdsm-catalogs, mainstream feminist magazines and punk zines, the material included in this list is curated based on (subjective) criteria. I have selected zines, magazines, newspapers, artist's books etc, while equally looking at content and design. How the cover looks, the use of typography and photography, layout,…

Post-porn is not limited to explicit imagery at all. It's better understood as a political and cultural stance, one that resists normative representations of sexuality, body and gender, and actively creates space for alternative narratives. Some magazines such as HERESIES, GIRLIE MAG, or THE LESBIAN TIDE, prioritize political discourse over explicit content.

Although the "official" post-porn movement is often situated in the 1980s, earlier printed materials played a crucial role in shaping the vision. Feminist, queer, and anti-racist struggles created the space in which post-porn could emerge. The material in this index ranges from, my oldest reference being a 1920s lesbian magazine DIE FREUNDIN, to contemporary publications like PRECIOUS LIQUID, HONK!, PORNCEPTUAL, or BADSEEDZINE. This way we look at what came before (pre-post-porn), what was during the post-porn movement, and what came after (post-post-porn).

The movement sparked in the United States, but emerged in other countries as well, making this a global phenomenon. That is why the material wanders from the U.K. to Germany, from Italy to Sweden and the Netherlands, and back to the U.S.A.

Before digital networks, printed matter operated as a vital infrastructure for excluded communities. Magazine, zines, and newsletters functioned as tools for communication, publication became a major life-line for its readers. Print functioned as a connection tool, getting in contact with and hearing from like-minded people.

"The presence of magazines was very much part of everyday life. Long before we had access to other kinds of media, the printed page was our primary source—they were such an integral part of our visual culture."[8]

INSPIRATION: CYBERFEMINISM INDEX

The concept of this index is inspired by Mindy Seu's iconic 'Cyberfeminism Index'. Started in 2019 as an open source, open-access, crowd-sourced spreadsheet that turned into an online database, and eventually was published as a book in 2023. Seu describes her index as a toolkit that organizes, connects, and cites material from hundreds of projects.

By adopting the form of an index—traditionally understood as a neutral tool of organization—Seu reconfigures it into a dynamic, networked structure that resists singular authority. Even though this index serves as a subjective archiving project (me being the only one who curates), it doesn't thrive on authority or hierarchy. This project is not necessarily about my voice, but about the voices of a previous generation, my chosen ancestors.

ON INDEXING AND NEUTRALITY

Indexes and other data visualization structures, often claim to be objective and complete. They are a flawed tool of oppression, seeming to present an expert, neutral point of view, while in reality reproducing existing structures of authority and exclusion. According to designer Sheila Levrant de Bretteville, every form of ordering is a "situated practice", rooted in experiences, perspectives, and social positions.

Her 'feminist design'-ethics and theory allow us to view indexes no longer as neutral data sets, but as subjective and politically charged structures. "Feminist design", according to de Bretteville, "looks for graphic strategies that will enable us to listen to people who have not been heard before."[9] Feminist indexes do not strive for a singular universal truth, but rather for relational knowledge. This index becomes a tool for amplifying voices that have historically been excluded.

THE CARRIER BAG THEORY

This project, like Seu's 'Cyberfeminism Index', makes a clear connection to Ursula K. Le Guin's essay 'The Carrier Bag Theory of Fiction' (1986), in which the author argues that the first human tool was not the weapon or the spear, but "a leaf a gourd a shell a net a bag a sling a sack a bottle a pot a box a container. A holder. A recipient." A "container" for holding and possibly storing something. Seu explicitly positions herself as a gatherer, echoing Le Guin's ethos of collecting, storing, connecting, rather than isolating. She words it as, "Gathering, for Le Guin, is not a masculine, techno-utopian process of disruption or of moving fast and breaking things, but the methodical, deep labor that comes from 'looking around, rather than looking ahead,' from gathering rather than hunting."

ACTIVATING THE MATERIAL

This Post Porn Print Index does not solely exist as a digital structure, being this website. It will also be published as an annex to the connected post-porn magazine Cuntish Magazine—issue #1 coming in June 2026. In this way, I am activating the material again through print, returning to the very medium it documents. This printed annex will be out-of-date almost immediately when printed, but this website will be kept up-to-date as long as I run the magazine.

What I keep wondering is that, is it actually a good thing that queer publications are becoming more mainstream? Or does that shift in position risk diluting something essential about "queerness", making it less radical? The sense of living on the edges of society, it has been a condition imposed from the outside, but what if now, it has become intertwined with how queerness understands itself? Marginality is not an inherent trait of queerness, but neither is it easily left behind, it keeps lingering.

Inès De Laender
